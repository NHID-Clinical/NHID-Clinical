About NHID-Clinical

An open governance proposal for transparent healthcare voice AI.

NHID-Clinical was initiated by Brianna Baynard based on operational experience in healthcare payer operations at United Concordia Dental (Highmark Health). This is a solo-authored proposal seeking feedback, validation, and adoption from healthcare AI voice platforms and payers.

What it does

What NHID-Clinical Does

Initiated by Brianna Baynard. No formal organization or accreditation body exists behind it yet. This is a voluntary open governance proposal — not an official standard or regulatory requirement.

NHID-Clinical is a voluntary governance proposal focused on one specific problem: AI voice agents in healthcare workflows that solicit data before clearly disclosing they are non-human. This creates "impersonation latency" — the gap between call start and human realization, leading to hang-ups, trust breakdown, and potential PHI exposure.

The creator witnessed this pattern daily in payer operations: AI agents using human names like "Sarah," collecting NPI and member IDs across a full call workflow, then disclosing AI identity only when challenged by suspicious staff — long after protected data had already been exchanged.

What this proposal provides

  • Deterministic conformance tests with clear pass/fail criteria (CTS IDG-01 through ATR-01)
  • A tiered alignment model (L1 self-attestation, L2 operational evidence, L3 independent audit) for early implementation testing
  • A shared disclosure vocabulary payers and providers can reference in vendor requirements
  • Open specification under CC BY 4.0 — free to implement and adapt

What this proposal is not

  • Not a HIPAA compliance solution. NHID-Clinical supports safer workflows; it does not replace HIPAA or address PHI access controls.
  • Not a broad enterprise NHI security framework. Scope is voice workflows in B2B payer–provider contexts — not endpoint security or identity federation.
  • Not required by law. This is a voluntary proposal. Adoption is driven by payer vendor requirements and platform differentiation.

The problem

Impersonation Latency

AI voice agents are calling payer customer service lines on behalf of providers — requesting claim status, eligibility details, and prior authorization updates. The agents give NPI numbers and member IDs to authenticate. Payer reps, following their script, provide the information. 3–5 minutes later, something feels off.

The rep asks: "Are you a real person?" The AI admits it's automated. The call is terminated. Protected operational data was already shared. There's no audit trail. The AI calls back 10 minutes later. Same cycle.

This is Impersonation Latency — the compliance and operational gap where payer systems have no standard for what a legitimate AI-initiated call looks like.

AI voice call enters the workflow
Rep provides operational data before identity check
3–5 minutes later: "Are you a real person?"
Call terminated. Data already shared. No audit trail.
NHID-Clinical: disclosure before any data exchange

Document layers

Three layers. Clear separation.

NHID-Clinical separates enforceable rules from guidance so organizations can adopt a practical minimum while preparing for deeper verification.

Layer 1 — Normative

Mandatory requirements

MUST/MUST NOT rules using RFC 2119 terminology. Five CTS tests (IDG-01 through ATR-01). The enforceable baseline for claiming conformance.

View CTS tests →
Layer 2 — Recommended

Best practices

SHOULD/MAY guidance for authorization verification, audit logging, and escalation UX patterns. Not required for certification but strongly encouraged.

Layer 3 — Informative

Future scope

FHIR technical mapping, badge schema, NHID-Auth companion spec, registry architecture (v1.4+), and JWT handshake implementation.

Scope

What NHID-Clinical covers

✓ In scope

  • B2B administrative voice workflows
  • Provider-to-payer AI agent calls
  • Claim status, eligibility, prior auth inquiries
  • Business Associate-to-payer workflows
  • Outbound AI agent disclosure requirements

✗ Not in scope (v1.3)

  • Direct-to-consumer or patient-facing calls
  • Clinical triage or diagnostic AI
  • Inbound call center AI (payer-to-provider)
  • Cryptographic handshake protocols (v1.4+)
  • Live public registry (v1.4+)

For platforms

How NHID-Clinical Works with Voice Platforms

Voice AI platforms provide the core engine and calling infrastructure. NHID-Clinical provides the independent safety, disclosure, and trust layer that payers and providers can rely on across platforms.

Think of NHID-Clinical as an open proposal — analogous to what OAuth is for identity delegation — rather than a competing platform. This proposal defines behavioral requirements: disclosure timing, authorization verification, and escalation protocols. Platforms implement; the community validates and refines.

Platforms built to meet NHID-Clinical requirements can demonstrate legitimate, auditable workflows that healthcare organizations can trust — turning disclosure compliance into a competitive differentiator rather than an operational burden.
