NHID-Clinical

A shared language for transparent AI voice agents in healthcare

AI voice systems are calling payer offices, verifying benefits, and exchanging sensitive information — often without ever saying they're automated. By the time a representative realizes they're talking to a machine, the conversation has already happened.

This is a voluntary open proposal by Brianna Baynard. Not a standard. Not a certification. Just a named problem and four suggested behaviors.

The problem

An AI voice agent calls a payer's office. It sounds professional. It provides provider credentials and asks about a claim. The representative answers — sharing details they would only share in a legitimate workflow.

Three minutes in, the rep gets suspicious: "Wait — are you actually a person?"

Only then does the system admit it's automated.

Operational data has been shared. There's no clear record of when disclosure happened. And there's no way to know whether the credentials were real or scraped from a public directory.

This is impersonation latency. It wastes time, erodes trust, and creates risk on both sides of the call. And it's happening in payer–provider workflows across healthcare daily.

What this proposal suggests

Four behaviors. No formal enforcement. No certification. Just a shared starting point.

Say you're not human — before anything else.

Identify as an automated system at the very start of the call, before any data is exchanged.

Sound like what you are.

No fake breathing, no scripted filler sounds, no behavioral patterns designed to make the system seem human.

Make it easy to reach a human.

When the person on the other end asks to speak with someone real, that should always be straightforward.

Keep a basic record.

When did the call start. When was the system identified as automated. What happened. Simple logs support accountability.
