Script Examples

What passing disclosure looks like.

Reference scripts for AI voice agents operating in B2B payer–provider workflows. Each example maps directly to proposed requirement IDG-01 (identity disclosure before data solicitation).

Proposed requirement: IDG-01

The agent MUST disclose its non-human identity at or before the first opportunity to solicit data. These examples demonstrate passing and failing patterns.

View all proposed requirements →

Section 1 — Overview

The disclosure requirement in plain language

The core NHID-Clinical requirement is simple: an AI voice agent must clearly state that it is non-human before asking for any protected or operational information. The disclosure must be the first substantive statement on the call — not buried three minutes in, and not offered only when challenged.

Timing

Disclosure must precede any data solicitation — NPI, member ID, claim number, or any identifier.

Clarity

The word "automated," "AI," "non-human," or equivalent must appear. Ambiguous phrasing does not pass.

Consent

The receiving party must be given an opportunity to accept, redirect, or terminate before the workflow begins.

Section 2 — Passing Script Openings

Example passing script openings

These openings pass IDG-01. Each states the calling organization, discloses non-human identity, and requests permission before proceeding. Organization and purpose are illustrative — substitute your actual system name.

General payer operations inquiry

"Hello, this is an automated AI voice agent from Acme Health Plan. I am not a human. I'm calling regarding a claim. Do I have permission to continue?"

Discloses: automated · non-human · purpose · permission request. Maps to IDG-01 pass.

Dental payer — claim status inquiry

"This is the AI claims assistant for Delta Dental. I am a non-human system. May I have your NPI to check a claim status?"

Discloses: non-human · organization · purpose. Data (NPI) is requested after disclosure. IDG-01 pass.

Prior authorization follow-up

"Good morning. This is an automated system calling on behalf of Riverside Medical Group to follow up on a prior authorization request. I am not a person — I'm an AI agent. Are you available to continue?"

Non-human disclosure precedes all data activity. Consent asked explicitly. IDG-01 pass.

Eligibility verification

"Hi, this is an automated voice agent — not a human — calling from Valley Provider Services to verify eligibility for a patient visit. Before I give you any details, do I have your permission to proceed?"

Disclosure first, purpose stated, permission requested. IDG-01 pass.

Section 3 — Failing Examples (Training Reference)

Patterns that fail IDG-01

These are real-world patterns observed in AI voice agent deployments. The first example is the most severe — the complete multi-test failure pattern that NHID-Clinical was built to prevent. Each subsequent example isolates a single correctable failure mode. Use all of these for internal training and QA against your own scripts.

Fail — The full impersonation latency pattern (most severe)
AI Agent "Hi, this is Sarah from Dr. Smith's office calling to check claim status for a patient. The provider's NPI is 1234567890."
Call center rep verifies NPI in system.
AI Agent "The member ID is 987654321, date of service was 01/15/2025." Multiple data points already exchanged — PHI/operational data exposed to an undisclosed system with no informed consent or audit trail.
Rep (suspicious) "Wait… are you an AI agent?"
AI Agent "Yes, I am Sarah, an automated voice system authorized by Dr. Smith to retrieve claim status."
Fails IDG-01 (no upfront disclosure), PDX-01 (data exchanged before disclosure), and DBC-01 (human name used without AI qualifier). This is the core Impersonation Latency pattern: appears human, collects sensitive data, admits AI identity only when challenged. By this point, NPI and member ID have been exposed to an undisclosed system. This pattern creates the exact trust breakdown NHID-Clinical was built to prevent.
Fail — No disclosure, human name used
"Hi, this is Sarah from claims. What's your member ID?" No disclosure that the caller is non-human. A human name implies a human caller. Data (member ID) is solicited immediately. Fails IDG-01.
Fail — Data solicited before disclosure
"Hello, may I have your claim number? By the way, I'm an AI." Disclosure occurs after data is requested. IDG-01 requires disclosure at or before the first data solicitation — "by the way" ordering fails the timing requirement.
Fail — Ambiguous disclosure buried mid-call
"Hello, I'm calling about claim number 84729. I need to verify the member's eligibility and date of service. [3 minutes later] I should mention this call may be automated." Disclosure buried mid-workflow after multiple data exchanges. This is the core Impersonation Latency pattern. Fails IDG-01 on timing.
Fail — Disclosure only on challenge
[Call proceeds for 4 minutes] Rep: "Wait — are you a real person?" Agent: "No, I'm an automated system." Disclosure triggered by human challenge rather than agent initiative. By this point protected data has been exchanged. Fails IDG-01 and PDX-01.

Section 4 — Where NHID Does Not Apply

Workflows outside this proposal's scope

NHID-Clinical v1.3 specifically addresses B2B administrative voice workflows between AI agents and healthcare payer operations. The following categories are outside scope — different risk profiles and disclosure frameworks apply.

  • Patient-facing appointment reminders AI-to-patient voice calls operate under different consent and disclosure rules (FTC, TCPA). NHID-Clinical does not govern direct-to-consumer healthcare calls.
  • Internal staff-to-staff voice tools Intra-organization AI voice tools used by clinical or administrative staff internally are outside scope. No payer–provider data exchange boundary is crossed.
  • Non-healthcare voice applications This proposal is specific to healthcare administrative workflows. Voice AI in other regulated industries (finance, legal) requires separate governance standards.
  • Text-based chatbots and messaging NHID-Clinical scope is voice. Chat-based AI systems that handle claims and eligibility via secure messaging portals are not covered by v1.3 requirements.
  • Inbound call center AI (payer-to-provider) Where the payer operates an inbound AI to answer provider calls, different operational dynamics apply. This workflow is under consideration for a future version.
  • Clinical triage and diagnostic AI AI systems that perform clinical decision support, triage, or diagnosis are governed by FDA and clinical AI frameworks, not NHID-Clinical.
