Operational Guide · 90-Day Pilot

For Payers: Running an NHID-Clinical Pilot

A practical 90-day structure for payer operations teams who want to test AI voice agent transparency requirements with one vendor before expanding.

Status: No payer has adopted NHID-Clinical yet. This is a reference guide for teams considering a first pilot. NHID-Clinical is not a formal standard or regulatory requirement — it is a voluntary proposal you can reference in vendor conversations if it fits your needs.

What You Get From a Pilot

Three measurable operational improvements, scoped to one vendor and one workflow:

  • Disclosure latency under 5 seconds. Staff know they are talking to an AI before any operational data is requested. Target: <5 seconds from call answer to identity disclosure.
  • Escalation friction reduced by >30%. Measure staff-initiated "transfer me to a human" requests before and after. The NHID-Clinical escalation behavior (EIT-01) should reduce friction because staff aren't fighting the system to reach a person.
  • Audit trail completeness. Every call produces a machine-readable event log with disclosure timestamp, escalation outcome, and PHI-access record. You can pull this log without calling the vendor.

90-Day Pilot Structure

Four phases. Each phase has a gate — you don't move forward until the gate passes.

Weeks 1–2 · Baseline

Pull 30 days of existing call logs from the vendor. Measure current disclosure latency, escalation rate, and audit completeness. This is your before-state. If the vendor cannot produce call logs for this period, the pilot ends here.

Weeks 3–6 · Configuration

Vendor configures their system against the four NHID-Clinical behaviors: upfront identity disclosure, no deceptive artifacts, immediate escalation path, and structured audit logging. You review configuration docs — not marketing copy. Gate: vendor provides written confirmation of which NHID-Clinical behaviors are implemented and which are not.

Weeks 7–10 · Live Observation

Live calls with NHID-Clinical configuration active. Your ops team logs anomalies in a shared sheet: calls where disclosure didn't happen before data exchange, escalation requests that stalled, audit fields missing. Gate: fewer than 5% of observed calls with disclosure failures.

Weeks 11–12 · Measurement & Decision

Compare Week 7–10 metrics against the Week 1–2 baseline. Decision: expand to additional workflows, pause for remediation, or exit the pilot. Write a one-page summary — this becomes your internal evidence base if you reference NHID-Clinical in future vendor contracts.

What this pilot is NOT for: This is not a compliance certification, a HIPAA audit, or a legal review of vendor contracts. NHID-Clinical has no enforcement mechanism. The pilot gives you operational evidence — what you do with that evidence is your decision.

RFP Language (Copy-Paste Ready)

The following clause names NHID-Clinical explicitly and uses SHALL as a contractual obligation. Adapt as needed — your legal team should review before use.

Vendor SHALL implement the four behavioral requirements defined in the NHID-Clinical v1.3 proposal (nhid-clinical.org/specification.html) for all automated voice agents contacting [Plan Name] staff on administrative workflows: 1. Identity disclosure before any operational data is exchanged. 2. No audio artifacts or behavioral patterns designed to simulate human presence. 3. Immediate, unambiguous transfer to a human operator upon request. 4. Structured event logging sufficient to reconstruct call content and timing. Vendor SHALL provide machine-readable audit logs conforming to the NHID-Clinical canonical event schema within 5 business days of request. Logs SHALL include disclosure_timestamp, escalation_outcome, and phi_accessed fields as defined in schema/nhid_trace_schema_v1.json. These requirements are defined by a voluntary open proposal, not a regulatory body. Their inclusion here creates a contractual obligation between the parties regardless of the proposal's standardization status.

Vendor Questionnaire

Four questions. Ask before you sign. Acceptable answers are noted.

  1. At what point in the call does your system identify itself as automated?
    Acceptable: "Before any data request, within the first utterance." Not acceptable: "When the human asks" or "It depends on the call script."
  2. What happens when a staff member says "transfer me to a human"?
    Acceptable: Specific transfer behavior, latency target, fallback if no human is available. Not acceptable: "We handle escalation" without specifics.
  3. Can you provide a sample audit log from a prior call, with disclosure_timestamp and escalation_outcome fields?
    Acceptable: Actual sample log (redacted). Not acceptable: "We log everything" without showing the schema.
  4. Does your system use any audio processing — breathing sounds, filler words, variable speech cadence — to reduce the perception of automation?
    Acceptable: "No" or explicit list of what is and is not used. Not acceptable: "Our voice sounds natural" without addressing the question directly.

Threat Model & Failure Handling

Four failure modes to plan for before the pilot starts:

Vendor claims compliance but cannot produce logs. Stop the pilot. Require log access as a prerequisite, not a deliverable.
Disclosure happens but is buried in a long opening script. The NHID-Clinical requirement is disclosure before data exchange, not disclosure somewhere in the first 60 seconds. If staff are answering PHI questions before they register the disclosure, the behavior fails regardless of the script.
Escalation path exists on paper but stalls in practice. Test it. Have a staff member request a human transfer during Week 7. If it takes more than 30 seconds or fails, log it as an EIT-01 failure.
Vendor updates their system mid-pilot. Require a change notification window (72 hours minimum). Undisclosed changes invalidate the baseline comparison.
